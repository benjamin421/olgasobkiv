What to Consider: Study Masters in Architecture in the US or UK?

I am writing this article because in retrospect I would have benefitted from some extra guidance regarding the above noted question. When I was researching this question there where no good sources that clearly outlined a decision making framework.

A little bit of background about me: I completed my Bachelor of Science in Architecture at Washington University in St. Louis, graduating in 2021. I was interested in the prospect of studying my Masters in Architecture in the UK for a while. After my first year of Undergraduate studies I came across the Bartlett School of Architecture through their Instagram. I visited London that summer briefly for 3 days and after that the Bartlett was set as a reach goal. I started to research the logistics regarding study in the UK at the beginning of my fourth and final year at WashU. There was no clear definitive resource online to give me conclusive answers so I reached out to several alumni, and read over both the US and UK architecture registration board websites. My conclusion was that a year out working would be required for me to have a chance at getting into UK architecture Masters programs. It was worded as ‘Not mandatory but highly encouraged’. Ultimately, I decided to apply to three schools in the US (Columbia, RICE, Harvard) and two in the UK (AA and the Bartlett). In the end it was a toss-up between RICE and the Bartlett, the US or the UK. My final decision was to venture to the UK. Soon enough I was in the UK and starting classes in the Bartlett. The Masters in Architecture program in the UK is a two-year program, each year consisting of 3-terms. After completing my first of three terms I decided to take an interruption. An interruption means that I currently have until the summer to decide whether or not I will be returning to the Bartlett next year. It was not an easy decision and hence I am now writing this blog to serve as a helpful resource to others considering a similar path. *In this article please keep in mind that I am assuming that you are in a similar position to myself where you have already completed your undergraduate in the US.
It is important to firstly understand that the US and the UK have a different process for becoming a registered architect. Therefore, if you are trying to decide whether or not this is a good idea for you, I would first and foremost ask whether getting licensed as architect is ultimately your end goal. If so, then ask yourself where you most principally want to practice? For me, which I will elaborate on a little farther down in this post, this was a large deciding factor for my interruption. If yes and to hold a license in the US, then I would highly recommend completing your Masters at a NCARB accredited US university and looking for opportunities abroad farther down the line in your career. If yes and you are not opposed to being a registered architect principally in the UK than this is a great route to doing so. If you don’t see yourself getting registered as an architect and see this more as a two-year adventure for a Masters in London then why not! London is a fantastic city to grow both professionally and personally, and if it weren’t for the realization that I may not be able to get licensed in the US I would likely still be there. I will now share with you an anecdote regarding a Bosnian lawyer that helped me come to this realization.

Bosnian Lawyer: My friends all went to law school in the US.
My friend: Why did you decide to study in the UK?
Bosnian Lawyer: I wanted to be different.
My friend: And…?
Bosnian Lawyer: I am still in the UK.
If this is as far as you read, then your one main takeaway should be that by studying your Masters in the UK does make your path to licensure in the US (if you want this as an available option) unnecessarily difficult, if not impossible. I don’t doubt that it is possible to eventually figure something out but there will 100% be many hoops to hop through in order to make it work. Not to mention, that the way the new reciprocity agreement between the US and UK has been announced would make it easier for you to first get licensed in the US and then get reciprocity in the UK rather than vice versa. To understand what I mean by this it helpful to understand the differences between the US and UK architecture licensure process if you are not yet familiar.

Getting Licensed in the UK

If you study or practice architecture in the UK than you will quickly become familiar with the ARB and RIBA. The ARB (Architects Registration Board) is the principle body in the UK regulating the registration of qualified ‘Architects’. RIBA (Royal Institute of British Architects) is conversely a noncompulsory but recommended organization that already registered architects join. Therefore, the ARB is the sole body responsible for the registration of architects whilst RIBA could be viewed as akin to the AIA, promoting continued education and high professional standards within architecture. The ARB has a three-part system to licensure which I outlined below. Although all of the core elements are the same - education, experience, and examination - the order in which they must be completed does differ significantly.
Part I: Undergraduate degree (3 years). Here is the RIBA list of validated schools and courses. Completing a RIBA validated course means means you automatically gain the Part I qualification when you graduate and forgo Part I examination. Part I: Professional Experience (1 Year). You’re expected to do is find a job in architecture for 1 year. Although this is not a rigid requirement many Masters degree programmes stipulate in their entry requirements. During your years of experience you are required to fill in records of your professional activities known as PEDRs (Professional Experience and Development Record). This is done through the RIBA here.
Part II: Masters Degree (2 Years). As with Part 1, validated courses give you exemption from the RIBA Part 2 Examination and give you automatic Part 2 qualification upon completion of your Masters degree.
Part II Professional Experience/ Part III Examination (1 Year): Complete the last 12 months of the total 24 required before you can take the Part 3 Examination. The whole exam process isn’t just a single exam, it’s made up of different bits, including among other things your completed PEDRs, a case study of a project you have lead, an interview, and a written exam.

Getting Licensed in the US

The US licensure process is similar, however it is not categorized into the 3-part system like the UK. Each of the 55 U.S. states set their own requirements for licensure that all include three core components - education, experience, and examination. If you are looking to get licensed in the US you should be familiar with the following terms - NAAB, AIA, NCARB, AXP, and ARE. The National Architectural Accrediting Board (NAAB) A degree from a NAAB-accredited school meets the education requirement to get certified by the National Council of Architectural Registration Boards (NCARB). NCARB-certification makes it easier to get licensed in other states, so you can work anywhere. Therefore NCARB is analogous to the ARB in the UK. The American Institute of Architects (AIA) is similar to the RIBA. The Architecture Experience Program (AXP) regulates the experience requirements for registered architects and the Architect Registration Examination (ARE) is the regulated examination. NCARB outlines the following requirements to Earn Your Architecture License.
Earn an architecture degree from an NAAB-accredited program, or meet education requirements of your jurisdiction.
Gain and document the required experience. All 55 U.S. jurisdictions accept the AXP (Architectural Experience Program) but some require additional experience. Experience is recoded through an NCARB record with a specified breakdown of hours.
Pass the ARE. required by all 55 jurisdictions.
Meet any additional juridical requirements. Some boards require additional experience or a location-specific supplemental exam before licensure.
Have your NCARB Record transmitted to the jurisdiction where you would like to be licensed. Your NCARB Record is a verified account of your professional history that demonstrates your qualifications for licensure

Why you may need to reconsider your decision to study your Master’s in the UK?

The similarities between the US and the UK architecture education and licensure systems can be deceiving. My own conclusion was , “they are similar enough that I can surely make it work.” However, there are some very clear hoops and hurdles that you should be aware of prior to making your decision.
Firstly, if you are in the same position as myself and have completed your Bachelor’s of Science in Architecture (or similar) at a US University, you will need to get Part 1 equivalence in the UK. Do note that if you choose to go down this route the prescribed exam will cost you at least £1950.00. I myself have not yet taken the Prescribed Examination so I cannot attest to the ease or difficulty of this process but if you want to read more about ARB Prescribed Exams for those who hold overseas or non-recognized UK qualifications here. Also, please note that you don’t have to get Part I Prescribed Equivalence to apply for the Master’s Program. It is in your best interest to get Part I equivalence through the prescribed exam as soon as you reasonably can once you are in the UK.
Secondly, Architects should soon have increased freedom practicing between the US and UK. You can read more about the reciprocity agreement between the US and UK here. However, my conclusion is that getting licensed in the US and then receiving reciprocity in the UK is more advantageous and more easily attainable than vice-versa. The United States and United Kingdom ratified a reciprocity agreement for architects in June. The agreement won’t be in-effect until early in 2023 because the ARB must first review, approve and sign the agreement before it goes into effect.
However, it is key to note that this reciprocity agreement is only for architects that followed the ‘standard’ path to licensure in either the US or the UK. In the US this would mean that you would have to have an architecture degree from a NAAB-accredited program. Therefore, if your hope was to complete your masters in the UK and then get licensed via the EESA path, according to the information currently announced regarding reciprocity, you likely won’t qualify.
The standard path to licensure in the UK, as I understand it, would also require that all three parts - Part I, Part II, and Part III - had been completed in the UK. Therefore, getting Part I equivalence via the ARB Prescribed Exams would likely be deviation from the ‘standard’ qualification path. The official rules of the reciprocity agreement have not been disclosed, but after talking to an NCARB representative this was the conclusion that was drawn. It is also worth noting that there is a clause mandating that the reciprocity agreement be re-evaluated 3-years after the agreement officially goes into effect. So yes, the terms of the agreement could change in the future.

So, if your goal is to ultimately be a licensed architect, there is currently little guarantee unless you fully commit to the US or the UK ‘standard’ path. Additionally, it is currently noted that each U.S. jurisdiction must individually determine if they will accept the Mutual Recognition Agreement. So whilst getting licensed in the UK will gives you reciprocity rights to the select number of US jurisdictions that accept the mutual recognition agreement, getting licensed in the US will grant to reciprocity rights to the whole of the UK in addition to New Zealand, Australia, Canada, and Mexico where reciprocity agreement are already in-place. Lastly, do considering the UK no longer gives you the advantage of the EU. Following Brexit in 2020, if you are a licensed architect in the UK, you can practice in the UK.

Prepare Yourself for More Self-guided Education

If the practicality and nuances of licensure don’t bother you and you are sold on completing your Masters in Architecture in the UK, then I wanted to provide one more bit of helpful advice - come prepared to pave your own educational path. I can’t speak to every Masters in Architecture program in the UK, however this was the lesson I learned from my first term at the Bartlett. Do note that after speaking to students at other Universities, this does seem to be an overarching theme in the UK.
The Educational curriculum in the UK and US has some notable differences. I discussed this with my studio tutors and it was acknowledged that the US has a much more ‘academic’ approach to the teaching of architecture than the UK. My experience in the US undergraduate architecture program was that there was more rigid structure in place in regards to project briefs, site, deliverables, etc. My experience in the UK graduate architecture program was a heavy emphasis on self-lead learning. This is not a grievance on the more open-ended architectural education in the UK, however it was a rather difficult adjustment for me given that I was not prepared for the format of teaching. I see the value of both educational models. The main take-away is that if you are going to the UK, especially the Bartlett, prepare for the self-led education model ahead of time. If I could have done my first term over again I would have come in prepared with a clear topic of interest that could be developed into a project throughout the year. Come prepared with an idea of your own design process. Having a clearly defined understanding of how you want to work through your topic of interest will do you a world of good. What I mean by this is having a base framework for how you plan to investigate to topic of interest. Do you want to make physical models or do you want to focus on a digital approach? Are you interested in a particular construction material or methodology? Etc. Just having a conception of these topics will put you in a much better position for a successful graduate experience. This is not to say that my peers all came in knowing exactly what they were interested in and exactly how they were going to develop their projects. Everyone comes in with varied backgrounds, skills, interests and tutors are there to help guide your project. My hope is to primarily vocalize that a level of self-determinism and self-guided interest will be expected of you and coming prepared for this will benefit you greatly.
